<p>One of the most rewarding things we do here at Cypress Prong Farms is experimenting with crops that are not normally seen in North Carolina. This could be anything from <a href="/blog/profiles-in-green-yukina-savoy/">yukina savoy</a>, an overlooked cold-hardy green vegetable, to the gorgeous blush heritage variety of cabbage <a href="/images/blog/profiles-in-green-japanese-negi/cavolo-san-michele.jpg" target="_blank" rel="noopener">San Michele</a> from the Trentino region in northern Italy. Or <a href="https://www.latimes.com/home/la-lh-spigariello-spigarello-seeds-plants-20130729-story.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Spigariello</a>, another import from Italy that is gaining favor in some U.S. markets. Last year, we trialed a novel variety of Negi onion, 'Shimonita', and were completely taken by its stellar performance.</p>

<p>Over the years, we have planted several types of Negi onion, all producing multi-tillered bunches of slender green stalks or "scallions". The white, single-stalked Shimonita Negi we harvested in January sold out immediately at the local farmers' market. This was a clue. It also piqued my curiosity about the large number of so-called "bunching" onions cultivated for food in Asia. What is the origin and taxonomy of these plants? Could they possibly fill a void in fresh, local winter produce in North Carolina and similar environments? How are they grown in their native lands?</p>

<div style="clear:both; margin: 0; padding: 0;"></div>

<p style="margin-bottom: 0.25rem;">The bunching onion, Negi in Japanese, has been cultivated for food throughout Asia since antiquity, from Siberia to the tropics, including Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines. Botanists have ordained the Negi onion, <em>Allium fistulosum</em>, in Latin nomenclature and positioned it within the Cepa subgenus, which also includes other important edible Allium crops (Table 1).</p>

<div style="text-align: center; margin: 0.5rem 0 1.5rem 0;">
<img src="/images/blog/profiles-in-green-japanese-negi/table-1.png" alt="Table 1: Taxonomic groupings within the genus Allium" style="width: 580px; margin-bottom: 0;">
</div>

<p><em>Allium fistulosum</em> is a perennial herbaceous plant that is normally grown as an annual crop. Leaves and flower stalks are hollow in cross-section (Figure 1), an easily observable trait that differentiates <em>Allium fistulosum</em> from close relatives onion, garlic, and leek.</p>

<p style="margin-bottom: 0.25rem;">Negi onion has a sweet, distinctive flavor that complements most other food ingredients. That is to say, unlike onions and garlic, it's mild enough to eat raw and doesn't overpower dishes prepared with it. Negi can be <a href="https://pogogi.com/what-heck-are-negi-onions" target="_blank" rel="noopener">grilled</a>, sliced raw for salads, or pickled. In Japan, Fujime (2012) reported that <em>Allium fistulosum</em> was produced on 22,700 hectares, ranking fifth in planted area, behind daikon radish. Demand for Negi in Japan is strong; therefore, production has gravitated toward <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WjQXMa39L_Y" target="_blank" rel="noopener">highly mechanized operations</a>.</p>

<div style="text-align: center; margin: 0.5rem 0 1.5rem 0;">
<img src="/images/blog/profiles-in-green-japanese-negi/flower-leaf-cross-section.jpg" alt="Figure 1: Allium fistulosum inflorescence and hollow leaf cross-section" style="width: 450px; margin-bottom: 0;">
<p style="font-size: 0.85rem; font-style: italic; margin: 0; text-align: justify;">Figure 1. <em>Allium fistulosum</em> inflorescence and (inset) hollow or 'fistulose' leaf cross-section, a characteristic for which the plant is named.</p>
</div>

<p>To further confuse things, <em>Allium fistulosum</em> has many local names: in China, <em>cong</em>; in Taiwan, <em>chung</em>; and in Korea, <em>pa</em>. In France, it's <em>ciboule</em> or <em>oignon de Strasburg</em>; and in English-speaking countries, Japanese bunching onion, Spanish onion, two-bladed onion, spring onion, scallion, among others. No doubt it's acquired vernacular names in other languages, too. Here, I'll reserve the proper name Negi because of my affinity for Japan. Back in the early 00s, I stopped over in Japan <em>en route</em> from Cambodia, staying in Ibaraki Prefecture with the family of a Japanese agronomist with whom I worked while at NCSU. He introduced me to the local farming scene, which has an artisanal quality compared to the broadacre farms here in North America. While there, I visited the prestigious Tsukuba Science City, immersed in Japanese food culture, and learned how to make bokashi. Since then, we've raised many Asian vegetables on our farm in North Carolina, mainly for personal use. Tokyo and central North Carolina share roughly the same latitude and a similar climate, so most Japanese crops thrive in this region.</p>

<p style="margin-bottom: 0.25rem;"> Returning to classification, or what botanists term taxonomy, the genus Allium is placed within the high-level monocot order, or "one" embryonic seed leaf division of flowering plants (the other division is dicot, or "two" embryonic seed leaves). When we think about monocots, grasses immediately come to mind. Grasses occupy the top spot in economic importance; it's no exaggeration to say that a world without grasses would be an impoverished and uninviting place. On the other hand, it's obvious that the genus <em>Allium</em> differs morphologically from, say, familiar grasses like maize (corn), wheat, and rice. I'll describe some of these traits later, but the important thing to note is that the genus <em>Allium</em> is more closely related, genetically, to monocots like the agave, yucca, or amaryllis lily than grasses.</p>

<p>Recent surveys (Takhtajan, 1997; Friesen et al., 2006) have placed the genus Allium in its own distinct family, the Alliaceae, and in the Order Asparagales, named after, you guessed it, the humble asparagus. Some classification systems place <em>Allium</em> within the lily or amaryllis family, so the consensus, at least among specialists who wrangle over these things, is far from settled. After the grasses, the Asparagales are the second most agriculturally important monocot order. The progenitor of <em>Allium fistulosum</em> is not known with certainty, but evidence suggests it may have originated in northwestern China, where a closely related wild species, <em>A. altaicum</em>, occurs. The global diaspora of <em>Allium fistulosum</em>, like all Alliums, has closely followed on the heels of human migration for good reason: it's an adaptable, savory, and easy-to-grow culinary ingredient with long-appreciated medicinal properties.</p>

<p>In Japan, Negi onions are further classified into three major groups according to ecological and utilization preferences (Inden and Asahira, 1990). The 'Kaga' group is grown in the coolest parts of Japan mainly for its white (etiolated) stalks; the 'Kujo' group in the warmest parts of Japan, and is favored for its leaf production; and the 'Senju' group, which is intermediate in preference and grown for its white stalks in central Japan, near Tokyo. A fourth group, the 'Yagura-Negi' is a winter dormant crop cultivated mainly in home gardens in warmer parts of Japan for its green leaves. Negi onion cv. 'Shimonita' belongs to the Kaga group. Other Negi onions we've tested include Tokyo Long White and He-Shi-Ko, both of which are tillering types.</p>

<p style="margin-bottom: 0.25rem;">    Here, we'll pause to explain some of the terminology surrounding onion traits in the <em>Allium</em> family. Generally, two types of Alliums are distinguished: bulbing and non-bulbing. Bulbing is a trait characteristic of onions, pearl onions, shallots, and garlic. Whereas, Negi onion, chives, and Chinese chives do not form true bulbs. True bulbs are made up of many overlapping layers of specialized swollen leaf sheaths and modified bladeless leaves (scales); the true stem is a small disc of tissue, sometimes called a basal plate, located at the interface between the leaf sheaths and roots (Figure 2a). Some diagrams, like Figure 2a, refer to sheathing leaf bases as "scales" while others say "bases". This nomenclature is confusing, but anatomically, they are all specialized leaf sheaths. Non-bulbing onions share similar anatomical features, but the leaf sheaths do not accumulate carbohydrates. Therefore, sheaths appear flattened and lengthen longitudinally along the stalk axis. When the sheaths overlap, they form a tightly packed, stalk-like <em>pseudostem</em>. The pseudostem is the principal edible part of non-bulbing Alliums like Negi onion and leeks (Figure 2b). A slight bulb-like thickening of the pseudostem at the base may occur in some Negi onions, while other cultivars have edible leaves and stalks. For brevity, the word 'stalk' is used herein in the vernacular sense to mean pseudostem.</p>

<p>Negi onions are exceptionally well adapted to a wide range of climates and ecological situations. We can attest to this fact here in North Carolina. In the spring of 2019, several plantings of Negi 'Shimonita' were established on Helena sandy loam at our farm. Two prolonged dry periods coupled with high temperatures occurred from mid-May to early June, and again in September. The intervening period was variable in precipitation, with periods of wetness and dryness, but it was hardly optimal for crop growth. Much of the dryland corn in our area was severely scorched. Our sweet corn plots required daily sprinkling, and even then, stress was evident in the smaller ears and in the number of kernel rows per ear harvested. But we didn't irrigate the Negi onions the full season. Aside from a pre-plant sprinkling of 10-10-10 fertilizer as recommended and two hand-hoedings, the crop received no coddling. Despite this neglect, Negi 'Shimonita' exhibited few signs of stress. By October, we had a mature crop of fat, succulent Negi stalks ready to harvest. Rana and Mor (2018) reported the widespread cultivation of <em>Allium fistulosum</em> in sub-Saharan Africa. They noted that established plants are tolerant of heavy rainfall, drought, and hot, humid conditions near Brazzaville and Kinshasa in Central Africa. Our experience with the Negi onion in North Carolina supports the plant's resilience under stress. Nevertheless, there are many challenges to cultivating Negi onion at a commercial scale. Most of these concern the specialized agrotechniques required to cultivate the crop.</p>

<p>The YouTube video linked above provides a fair representation of techniques used in Negi production by Japanese farmers. According to the video, it's a highly specialized operation requiring multiple pieces of machinery (please ignore the pesticide sprayer in that video; that's not how it should be done!). We have none of that here for obvious reasons. Few people in North America are familiar with the white-stalk Negi onion, so there's little commercial demand for it, and virtually no infrastructure is devoted to its production beyond the diminutive green bunching onions that populate supermarket produce shelves. That is a shame. We believe a strong market for Negi onion could be developed in North Carolina and are taking small steps at CP Farms in that direction.</p>

<p>Our first crop was produced from transplants raised in floatbed trays in a hoop house similar to tobacco (Figure 3). The reason for this is the growth habit of the Negi onion. Direct-seeded onions are relatively slow to germinate (7-10 days at 15°C), and their delicate seedlings compete poorly with weeds. Pre-plant incorporated herbicides such as Prowl (pendimethalin) can help control annual grasses and some broadleaf weeds in onion fields; however, their use in North Carolina is restricted. And chemical weed control certainly wouldn't be acceptable if you're growing organically. That leaves cultivation as the only option for weed control, so it's important for onion transplants to hit the ground running. The video above is somewhat misleading, though. Transplanting appears to be done in ~30 cm rows, but later clips, particularly those excrable sprayer shots, show row spacings much wider than 30 cm. It is unclear whether the techniques in this video depict a single, continuous operation or a compilation of clips from different farms growing Negi onions in Japan.</p>

<div style="clear:both; margin: 0; padding: 0;"></div>

<p>One peculiar agronomic trait of Negi onion is the tendency for leaf sheaths to elongate as the soil is mounded around the plant. Earthing up, or "hilling," the crop produces long-blanched onion stalks favored by Japanese consumers. In the Japanese technique, earthing up is performed two or three times per season, creating 30+ cm-high, near-vertical ridges. Exactly how these ridges are built up is unknown; obviously, a special tool is employed for this, but I've never seen it in operation. Leaf sheaths vary in length according to the Negi cultivar: cv. 'Shimonita' of the 'Kaga' group are not so long, whereas, cvs. 'Hakusyu' and 'Kuronobori' of the 'Senji' group grow very long. That said, selecting cultivars for sheathing ability under ridging is certainly a worthwhile goal for plant breeders.</p>

<p>We put 'Shimonita' transplants in a shallow furrow and covered them with soil via hand hoe since we have no specialized earthing-up equipment. I believe it would be possible to rig up plow sweeps to cut a furrow and disc hillers for in-season ridge building, but this is still a far cry from the advanced Japanese techniques depicted in the video. Uyeda et al. (2016) at the University of Hawaii at Manoa evaluated 20 commercial Negi onion varieties for fresh weight and stalk length, including cv. 'Shimonita'. Plant beds were prepared by cutting 25 cm-deep furrows with a Honda walk-behind tiller equipped with plow sweeps, followed by hand transplanting, as in our efforts. The width between plant rows was 1.2 m to allow for mechanical hilling every three weeks. The authors do not specify how hilling was applied; my guess is that they tracked the same Honda walk-behind tiller through the interrows of the plant.</p>

<div style="clear:both; margin: 0; padding: 0;"></div>

<p>Aside from earthing up, Negi onion is not particularly demanding in cultural preferences. According to Warade and Shinde (1998), a soil pH between 5.7 and 7.4 is suitable for Negi onion (6.0 to 6.5 is recommended in North Carolina, see the online <a href="https://content.ces.ncsu.edu/north-carolina-agricultural-chemicals-manual/fertilizer-use" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2020 Manual</a>). The same authors report fertilizer totaling 200, 150, and 175 kg/ha of N, P, and K, respectively, applied three to four times per growing season. This is at the high end of the recommendation for green onion production by NC State University Cooperative Extension, but we didn't apply anywhere near this amount and still produced a decent crop. More research on cultural practices for white stalk Negi is needed. We didn't detect any insect or disease problems in our Negi plots, although sources indicate attacks by numerous pests, including the infamous onion thrip, vector of tospoviral agents tomato spotted wilt virus (TSWV), and iris yellow spot virus (IYSV) (Warade and Shinde, 1998; Brewster, 2008) have been reported. TSWV is prevalent here in North Carolina, and IYSV is on the rise globally. Both diseases are highly damaging to Alliums.</p>

<p>We'll evaluate Negi onion cv. 'Shimonita' again this year. The seed is readily available in North America from multiple sources; we've procured Asian vegetable seeds from <a href="https://trueleafmarket.com/collections/kitazawa" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kitazawa</a> over the years and have always had good luck. You can also easily save your seed. I'd like to see more commercial white-stalk varieties from Japan, including F1 hybrids, made available in the U.S. However, that will not occur until demand increases. At Cypress Prong, we're doing our part to see that it does.</p>
